📚 Hydrocarbon Fundamentals

Hydrocarbon Definition & Classification

Definition: Organic compounds containing only Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H) atoms bonded together.

General Formula: CₘHₙ (varying ratios depending on structure)

Primary Classification by Saturation:

  • Saturated Hydrocarbons: Only C-C and C-H single bonds (Alkanes)
  • Unsaturated Hydrocarbons: Contains C=C or C≡C (Alkenes, Alkynes, Arenes)

Classification by Structure:

  • Acyclic (Open-chain): Linear or branched structure (no rings)
  • Cyclic: Contains one or more rings (cyclopropane, cyclopentane)
  • Aromatic: Contains benzene ring with delocalized π electrons

Classification by Functional Group:

  • Alkane (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂): Single C-C bonds - fully saturated, inert
  • Alkene (CₙH₂ₙ): One C=C double bond - unsaturated, reactive
  • Diene (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂): Two C=C double bonds (conjugated or isolated)
  • Alkyne (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂): One C≡C triple bond - highly unsaturated, very reactive
  • Arene: Benzene ring C₆H₅- group - aromatic, stable resonance

Molecular Structure & Nomenclature

🔗 ALKANE (Saturated - C-C single bonds)

• General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (acyclic) or CₙH₂ₙ (cyclic)

• Structure: Only C-C and C-H single bonds (σ bonds only)

• Hybridization: Carbon sp³ hybridized, tetrahedral geometry

• Nomenclature Rules:

  • Prefix based on carbon count: meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-, hex-, hept-, oct-, non-, dec-
  • Suffix: -ane (e.g., methane, ethane, propane)
  • Branched: Use position numbers and branch names (e.g., 2-methylpropane)
  • Example nomenclature: C₄H₁₀ = butane; C₅H₁₂ = pentane; (CH₃)₄C = neopentane

• Examples: CH₄ (methane), C₂H₆ (ethane), C₃H₈ (propane), C₄H₁₀ (butane)

🔗 ALKENE (Unsaturated - C=C double bond)

• General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ (acyclic) or CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (cyclic)

• Structure: One or more C=C double bonds (σ + π bonds)

• Hybridization: Carbon sp² hybridized, trigonal planar geometry

• Nomenclature Rules:

  • Suffix: -ene (e.g., ethene, propene, butene)
  • Position number: Lowest number for double bond (e.g., 2-butene, 1-pentene)
  • Isomerism: cis-trans isomerism (e.g., cis-2-butene, trans-2-butene)
  • Multiple bonds: Diene (two C=C), triene (three C=C)

• Examples: C₂H₄ (ethene), C₃H₆ (propene), C₄H₈ (1-butene or 2-butene)

🔗 ALKYNE (Unsaturated - C≡C triple bond)

• General Formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (acyclic) or CₙH₂ₙ₋₄ (with ring)

• Structure: One or more C≡C triple bonds (σ + 2π bonds)

• Hybridization: Carbon sp hybridized, linear geometry (180° angle)

• Nomenclature Rules:

  • Suffix: -yne (e.g., ethyne, propyne, butyne)
  • Position number: Lowest number for triple bond (e.g., 1-butyne, 2-butyne)
  • Acidity: Terminal alkynes (HC≡C-R) are weakly acidic, form metal acetylides
  • Multiple triple bonds: Not common in natural compounds

• Examples: C₂H₂ (ethyne/acetylene), C₃H₄ (propyne), C₄H₆ (1-butyne)

⭕ ARENE (Aromatic - Benzene ring)

• Structure: 6-membered benzene ring (C₆H₅- as core group) with delocalized π electrons

• Hybridization: Carbon sp² hybridized, planar hexagonal geometry

• Resonance: Stabilized by resonance (4 kJ/mol more stable than expected)

• Nomenclature Rules:

  • Core: C₆H₆ = benzene (parent structure)
  • Monosubstituted: Benzene suffix (e.g., methylbenzene, chlorobenzene)
  • Disubstituted: Position numbers 1,2- (ortho), 1,3- (meta), 1,4- (para)
  • Common names: Toluene (methylbenzene), xylene (dimethylbenzene)

• Examples: C₆H₆ (benzene), C₇H₈ (toluene), C₈H₁₀ (xylene)

Chemical Properties & Reaction Mechanisms

🔗 ALKANE (Low reactivity - Substitution & Oxidation)

  • Complete Combustion: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + (3n+1)/2 O₂ → n CO₂ + (n+1) H₂O + Heat (ΔH < 0, exothermic)
  • Free Radical Halogenation: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (UV light, 3 steps: initiation, propagation, termination)
  • Cracking: Long-chain alkanes break into shorter hydrocarbons (heat, catalyst, pressure)
  • Stability: NO reaction with Br₂ (water), KMnO₄, H₂SO₄ under normal conditions
  • Molecular Reactivity: Increases with chain length; primary > secondary > tertiary carbons

🔗 ALKENE (High reactivity - Addition Reactions)

  • Addition of Halogen (Br₂): C=C + Br₂ → C-Br-C-Br (addition across π bond, brown color decolorizes)
  • Oxidation with KMnO₄: Breaks C=C to form diols (or carboxylic acids if terminal), purple decolorizes
  • Hydrogenation: C=C + H₂ → C-C (Ni/Pt/Pd catalyst, 100-200°C, reduces unsaturation)
  • Hydration (Markovnikov's Rule): C=C + H₂O → C-OH-C (H₂SO₄ catalyst, H adds to C with more H atoms)
  • Polymerization: nC₂H₄ → -(CH₂-CH₂)ₙ- (heat, pressure, catalyst, forms polyethylene)
  • Reaction Speed: Much faster than alkanes due to accessible π electrons

🔗 ALKYNE (Extreme reactivity - Multiple Additions)

  • Sequential Hydrogenation: C≡C + 2H₂ → C-C (two-step: triple bond → double bond → single bond)
  • Addition of Halogens: C≡C + 2Br₂ → CBr₂-CBr₂ (decolorizes brown color twice)
  • Hydration (Keto-Enol Tautomerism): HC≡CH + H₂O → CH₃CHO (HgSO₄ catalyst, forms acetaldehyde)
  • Terminal Alkyne Acidity: HC≡C-R + Na → Na⁺C≡C-R + ½H₂ (terminal H weakly acidic, pH ~25)
  • Acetylide Formation: HC≡C-R + Ag(NH₃)₂⁺ → AgC≡C-R (white precipitate, used for identification)
  • Reaction Speed: Fastest of hydrocarbons; both π bonds accessible for attack

⭕ ARENE (Aromatic Stability - Electrophilic Substitution)

  • Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution: C₆H₆ + Br₂ (FeBr₃ catalyst) → C₆H₅Br + HBr (substitution, not addition)
  • Nitration: C₆H₆ + HNO₃ (H₂SO₄ catalyst) → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O (produces nitrobenzene)
  • Friedel-Crafts Alkylation: C₆H₆ + RCl (AlCl₃) → C₆H₅-R + HCl (benzene attacks alkyl carbocation)
  • Friedel-Crafts Acylation: C₆H₆ + RCOCl (AlCl₃) → C₆H₅-CO-R + HCl (adds carbonyl group)
  • Aromatic Stability: Benzene highly stable (resonance stabilization, π electrons delocalized)
  • NO Reaction with: Br₂ (no catalyst), KMnO₄, H₂, simple oxidizing agents
  • Directing Effects: Electron-donating groups (OH, NH₂) activate benzene (ortho/para directors)
  • Deactivating Groups: Electron-withdrawing groups (NO₂, CN) deactivate benzene (meta directors)

Applications, Synthesis Methods & Industrial Uses

🔗 ALKANE APPLICATIONS

Common Uses:

  • CH₄ (Methane): Natural gas (heating, electricity, chemical synthesis)
  • C₃H₈ (Propane): LPG cylinders, heating fuel, cooking
  • C₅-C₁₂ (Petrol): Gasoline for vehicles, solvents
  • C₁₂-C₁₈ (Diesel): Heavy vehicle fuel, heating oil
  • C₁₈-C₃₀ (Fuel oil): Industrial heating, ship fuel, lubricants
  • Paraffin Wax: Candles, cosmetics, sealing agent

Industrial Synthesis/Production:

  • Fractional Distillation: Crude oil → heated to 350-400°C → vaporized → fractionating column (temperature gradient) → products separate by boiling point
  • Hydrogenation: CₙH₂ₙ + H₂ → CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (Ni catalyst, 150-200°C, increases saturation)
  • Coal Liquefaction: Coal + H₂ (catalyst, heat, pressure) → liquid hydrocarbons

🔗 ALKENE APPLICATIONS

Industrial Uses:

  • C₂H₄ (Ethene): Fruit ripening hormone (released by plants), polyethylene (PE) plastic production, antifreeze, anesthetics
  • C₃H₆ (Propene): Polypropylene (PP) containers, packaging, fibers, acrylic production
  • C₄H₈ (1,3-Butadiene): Synthetic rubber for tires, gaskets, tubing
  • Isoprene (C₅H₈): Synthetic rubber production, tire components

Industrial Synthesis/Production:

  • Dehydrogenation: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ → CₓH₂ₓ + CᵧH₂ᵧ₊₂ (500-600°C, catalyst, heat, pressure → removes H₂)
  • Ethanol Dehydration: C₂H₅OH → C₂H₄ + H₂O (170-180°C, H₂SO₄ or Al₂O₃ catalyst, removes water)
  • Cracking: Long-chain alkanes → smaller alkenes + alkanes (heat + catalyst, breaks C-C bonds)
  • Ethene Production from Naphtha: Steaming of naphtha at 800-900°C → produces ethene, propene, butadiene

🔗 ALKYNE APPLICATIONS

Industrial Uses:

  • C₂H₂ (Ethyne/Acetylene): Welding (~3000°C flame), metal cutting torches, chemical synthesis, carbide welding
  • Chemical Synthesis: Vinyl chloride (PVC plastics), acrylonitrile (acrylic fibers), acrylic acid
  • Explosives: Trinitrotoluene (TNT) synthesis via acetylene derivatives
  • Pharmaceutical Precursor: Used in synthesis of vitamins and medicines

Industrial Production Method:

  • Calcium Carbide Method: CaO + 3C → CaC₂ + CO (2500°C electric arc furnace, high temperature)
  • Hydrolysis: CaC₂ + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + C₂H₂↑ (room temperature, water liberates acetylene gas)
  • Partial Oxidation of Natural Gas: CH₄ + O₂ (catalyst, high temp) → C₂H₂ + H₂O (modern industrial method)

⭕ ARENE APPLICATIONS

Industrial Uses:

  • C₆H₆ (Benzene): Industrial solvent, pharmaceutical synthesis, pesticides, dyes, detergents, explosives (TNT)
  • C₇H₈ (Toluene/Methylbenzene): Paint thinner, solvent, TNT (trinitrotoluene) explosive, polyurethane foam
  • C₈H₁₀ (Xylene/Dimethylbenzene): Industrial solvent, lacquers, varnishes, synthetic fiber production, dye solvent
  • C₁₀H₈ (Naphthalene): Mothballs, dyes, lubricants, phthalic anhydride synthesis
  • Phenol (C₆H₅OH): Disinfectants, plastics (Bakelite, epoxy), adhesives
  • Aniline (C₆H₅NH₂): Dyes (azo dyes), pharmaceuticals, herbicides

Industrial Production/Extraction:

  • Oil Reforming: Naphtha fraction (100-200°C boiling point) → catalytic reforming with Pt catalyst (350-500°C) → produces aromatic compounds
  • Distillation of Coal Tar: Coal tar (by-product of coal coking) → fractional distillation → benzene, toluene, naphthalene separated
  • Acetylene Trimerization: 3C₂H₂ → C₆H₆ (200-300°C, Fe or Ni catalyst, produces benzene from acetylene)
  • Dealkylation: Toluene/xylene → benzene removal of methyl groups (hydrogen at high temp)
